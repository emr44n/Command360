# Command360 — International Markets

*xVR/LearnPro is a Dutch-rooted, internationally sold product (it cites 300+ organisations across 50+ countries). That proves two things: the demand exists globally, and the frameworks Command360 maps to are international standards, so the content ports abroad with mostly cosmetic change. This file maps where the demand sits, who already buys (xVR's named roster = your warm-target list), and how to sequence entry.*

> **Caveat:** the client names below are drawn from xVR's own published "users" pages and prior research; treat as a prospecting list to verify, not confirmed facts for public copy.

---

## 1. Why international is a real prize, not a distraction
- **The standards are international.** ICAO Annex 14 (aviation), the IMO/port-safety regime (maritime), NATO STANAGs (defence), and the Seveso Directive (the EU parent of COMAH) apply across dozens of countries. The aviation, maritime and major-hazard content you generate for the UK is ~90% reusable elsewhere — swap signage, language and a few assets.
- **xVR has already done the market education.** Every country where xVR sells is a country whose services already accept simulation-based command training. You're not creating a category, you're offering a cheaper, hardware-free tier into a proven one.
- **The private/industrial angle scales globally.** Airports, COMAH/Seveso sites, ports and private fire providers (Falck operates in many countries) exist everywhere with the same fast-procurement, no-employer-conflict advantage as in the UK.

## 2. The strategic caution: don't fight xVR on its home turf first
xVR is strongest in the **Netherlands and Northern Europe** — that's its fortress and where its relationships are deepest. Leading there means fighting the incumbent at its strongest. Better to win first where xVR is present but not dominant **and** where there's no language barrier, build proof and community, then expand.

## 3. Entry sequencing (easiest → hardest)
**Tier A — English-language, low-friction (target first):**
Republic of Ireland, New Zealand, Australia, Canada, Singapore, the Gulf states (UAE, Saudi, Qatar — heavy aviation/energy spend), and parts of the Caribbean/Africa using UK-style frameworks. No UI translation needed; standards are familiar; procurement is often faster than the UK.

**Tier B — Northern Europe (high English proficiency, xVR-saturated):**
Nordics, Germany, Switzerland, Benelux. Appetite is proven, but this is xVR's heartland — enter with a sharp price/feature wedge and ideally a local partner, not cold.

**Tier C — Wider EU & beyond (needs localisation):**
France, Southern/Eastern Europe, Baltics, etc. Worth it once the product supports UI translation and you have reference customers; the Seveso/ICAO/port standards still carry the content.

## 5. How to approach international (same playbook, adapted)
- **Same wedge:** cheaper than VR, browser-based, no hardware, plus the regulator-ready evidence export — mapped to the *local* equivalent standard (ICAO for aviation everywhere; Seveso for EU major-hazard; national port codes for maritime).
- **Partner, don't parachute.** International training providers and the private fire/industrial players (Falck especially) already hold the relationships and run the exercises — sell *through* or *with* them rather than direct.
- **Productise language.** UI translation is the main localisation cost; the scenario *logic* and most assets are reusable. Treat multilingual UI as a roadmap feature that unlocks Tier C.
- **Lead with the English-speaking aviation/energy buyers in the Gulf and Asia-Pacific** — high budgets, mandated training, and no localisation friction.

## 6. One-line framing for the prize
You've effectively been building a **globally applicable product while planning a UK launch**: the same engine, the same evidence pack, and content built against international standards. Keep that in view when sizing the opportunity and when deciding how much to invest in translation and partnerships — the addressable market is an order of magnitude larger than the UK alone.

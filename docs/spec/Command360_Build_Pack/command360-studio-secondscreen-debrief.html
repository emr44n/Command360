Studio feel · second screen · debrief

Make it feel like a pro tool — and let the candidate's phone join in.

You've seen XVR's back end — capable, but a cluttered grey panel from another era. This is the opposite direction: a modern, thick-framed, FX-lit studio that borrows its feel from Premiere, After Effects and Framer, not from XVR. Plus the genuinely new bits — the object inspector, a real layers panel, multi-angle scenes, a phone second-screen, and an AI debrief.

Studied your 5 XVR screenshots · researched second-screen + screenshot-blocking for the honest answers below.

The feel, not the layout

Don't copy XVR. Borrow from the tools editors already love.

No copyright worry and a far better product if the look comes from modern creative software, not a 2010s simulator. The reference points: Premiere / After Effects for the timeline and layer logic, Framer / Linear for the clean dark surfaces and thick rounded frames, Figma / Photoshop for the canvas, layers and inspector. The signature: heavy, confident panel frames; generous space; and FX glow that lights up only what's live.

Find the thing that's buried

A real Layers panel — straight from Photoshop.

XVR's "Placed items" is a grey tree with tiny locate icons. Yours should be the Layers panel everyone already knows: grouped, searchable, each row a thumbnail with show / lock / locate, drag to reorder front-to-back. When a layer's hidden under ten others, you find it in a second.

Two more names you asked for

The fire-plus-smoke thing, and the two training types.

Same approach as before — options with plain explainers, my pick highlighted.

Fire + smoke saved as oneTwo layers that look great together, joined so they drop in and play as a single thing.
Combo·Rig·Set·Bundle↳ Combo — "drop in the window-fire combo." Plain and obvious. Rig if you want the film/VFX feel; Set or Bundle for something neutral. A Combo can carry its own Look and play its layers linked.
Self-led, not real-timeNo instructor present — the candidate works through injects and questions, gets stats at the end.
Drill·Solo·Open Assessment↳ Drill — "send them a drill to complete." Self-paced, branching, scored. (This is the module your spec calls the questions-and-stats type.)
Live, instructor-drivenReal-time — the instructor fires injects as the candidate makes decisions. This whole tool.
Live Exercise·Hot Seat·Command Studio↳ Live Exercise is clear; Hot Seat is the authentic command-training term for the candidate in the decision seat. Keep Command Studio as the builder's name.
"I want to go to the rear"

One incident, several Views — front, rear, side, from the cab.

A house fire isn't one picture. Build a scenario as a set of Views — each its own base plate with its own layers and injects — and the instructor cuts between them when the candidate moves: "rear of the property" switches the Stage to the rear View. Authoring feels like building slides, but each View is a live, layered scene you can drop fire and crews onto.

How instructors build them: like a slide deck, but spatial — add a View, drop in its plate, place the fire/smoke/crews, attach injects. For a true walk-around moment, that's where the World-Labs 3D "step inside" button slots in later — but for the live flow, switchable flat Views are faster, cheaper and reproducible.

The phone as a second screen

Scan once. The instructor pushes things to their hand.

This is a genuinely classy, current idea — and it's how live events and broadcasts already work in 2026: an on-screen QR opens a companion page in the phone's browser, no app to download, and the operator pushes content to it live, in sync with the main screen. For you that means maps, incident boards, and 999 bystander footage landing on the candidate's phone the moment you decide.

Use it for

Maps · boards · 999 footage

Push the incident map so they can pan it on their phone. Drop a board or card to study. Play bystander 999 video on their device during the "journey" to the scene. Each one appears on cue, exactly when you trigger it.

When the session ends

It vanishes — reliably

The page is tied to a one-time, session-scoped token. End the session (or close the card) and the server revokes it; the phone goes blank. This part is rock-solid because it's controlled server-side, not on the device.

!

The honest screenshot answer you asked for: in a normal mobile browser you cannot reliably stop a screenshot — that's a hard platform limit even Netflix can't beat without DRM. Screen-capture blocking is only guaranteed in a native app (Android's FLAG_SECURE blocks it outright; iOS uses FairPlay DRM and screenshot detection).

Tier 1 · web, now

Expire + watermark

Frictionless QR page, content revoked on session end, with the candidate's name + timestamp burned across it. Can't stop a screenshot, but it's worthless to share and traceable. Ships first.

Tier 2 · web, partial

Detect & blank

Where the platform allows, blur or blank the card on a screenshot/recording or when the app loses focus. Partial coverage — a deterrent, not a guarantee.

Tier 3 · native app

True screenshot-proof

If a service demands hard protection, a small companion app makes it genuinely impossible to capture. Build it only if a buyer requires it.

✦

Beyond maps and video — what else the phone unlocks: it can be the candidate's private channel for a confidential SITREP or casualty card; a quick-response pad for the Question inject (they tap their decision, it drives the branch); a personal radio-log they keep; or a silent "request resources" panel. The phone becomes the candidate's tactical device while the big screen stays the scene.

Where the learning lands

Every decision, timestamped — then summarised by AI.

Your spec wants debrief and analytics, and your code already records the session. Surface it as a proper Debrief: a timeline of what was injected and when, the candidate's decisions against the scenario clock, the objectives they hit or missed, and an AI write-up that turns the log into plain-English feedback an assessor can sign off.

Because every trigger and decision is already timestamped to the session, the metrics fall out for free: time-to-first-action, decisions per minute, objectives hit/missed, and the full replay scrubber. The AI just turns that log into language — it never invents; it summarises what the timeline already proves.

Where this leaves us

A pro-tool feel, a phone in the loop, and a debrief that writes itself.

None of this is exotic: the inspector and layers panel are standard editor patterns, the second screen is a session-scoped web page pushed live (with an honest screenshot story), Views are switchable plates, and the debrief is your existing recording, surfaced and summarised. It all sits on what you've already built — and it's distinctly yours, not XVR's.
